The Studio

DHYAI is a material and surface design practice based in Bengaluru. It works across objects, textiles, and material studies for homes, interiors, private events, gifting, and hospitality.

The name ध्यै is Sanskrit — to fix the mind upon.
A fold. A weight. A softened edge. A repeated touch.
Some objects mark a moment. Others remain after the moment has passed. Dhyai works with the latter.

Each piece begins with material. Weight, texture, density, and the way a surface interacts with light are treated as primary conditions. Form is derived from these conditions. The work begins with how an object holds: through handling, wear, and continued presence within a space. Objects are developed for use, handling, and retention.

The studio focuses on the intersection of technical product engineering, architectural structure, surface rhythm, and tactile permanence.

The work is produced in small numbers, by hand, in workshops the studio has chosen to work with over time.
Nothing is hurried.

Material

Matter holding time.

Textile

DHYAI works with all textiles: pure cotton, muslin, silk and more. The fabrics are chosen for how they drape, how they hold shadows, and how they soften with lived experience.

Textile object construction and design extend multiple techniques, both modern and traditional. Be it hand block print, kantha stitch, age old madhubani painting or modern digital prints rooted in conceptual design language.

The current studies are not surface decoration. They are object families — bedsheets, bedcovers, dohars, table covers, table linen sets — developed through repetition, spacing, weight, and use.

Each design is born out of reason and experiential phenomenon. It is a memory. It is a moment. It is a monument.

Cast Brass

Intimate intellectual and spiritual experiences bring out the conception of objects that are then translated into the grammar of material. Material is only a medium. And material such as brass, embodies that grammar most generously.

The brass develops a living patina over time, where the surface deepens with use. And the weight of the material is essential to its presence in a room.

Brass pieces are sand-cast, lathe-finished, and satin-brushed.

Current brass work includes the Pebble Jār Set and the Kālāsh. Both are made for household use, gifting programmes, and custom adaptations.

Study One — Sthāna

स्थान · to be housed

A rose has been climbing the brick wall for some years. The brick holds it, and the rose holds the brick.

Sthāna is the cloth that comes from looking at this for long enough.

Hand-block-printed in cotton, in a single field of greens, with six to seven rose medallions held within the brick lattice.

Where Sthiti speaks, Sthāna simply occupies.

For rooms where the cloth should feel like it was always there.

Formats: bedsheet, bedcover, dohar, table cover, table linen set.

Study Two — Sthiti

स्थिति · the held moment

The same wall, on the day of a celebration.

The roses are still there. The brick is still there. Colour has entered the field because something in the house has shifted.

A design where colour and form have stopped moving against each other and simply arrived.

A grid that holds its ground.
A floral medallion that punctuates, not decorates.

The bedroom.
The dining table.
The room you return to without thinking.

Formats: bedsheet, bedcover, dohar, table cover, table linen set.

Study Three — Punār

पुनर् · again

A pause taken beneath a tall palm.

The eye looks up, looks again.

Punār is the cloth that records the second look, when the rhythm slows. The palm sits inside a more openly spaced rectangular structure.

The dohar reverse carries a dot grid — a quieter field on the face the eye does not always see.

Suited to rooms where surfaces need to read with more air.

Formats: bedsheet, bedcover, dohar.

Pebble Jār Set

Two jars that sit in the hand, like pebbles taken from a riverbank.

The tray is the flat rock one carries home to place the pebbles.

Solid brass. Satin-brushed. Made for table use — salt, condiments, dry spices, small offerings.

A pebble from a riverbed — held in the hand once, understood completely.

The patina is the accumulation of every time the object was picked up.

Kālāsh

कलश · a vessel

A form that has existed in this part of the world for as long as there has been a part of the world.

Cast in solid brass, satin-brushed, with a single engraving on the belly.

Made for puja, household ritual, and everyday presence.

Water in brass changes.
The vessel holds that change.

A full belly, a cast shoulder, a steadiness that requires no explanation.

Commissions

Commissions are how the studio enters another house.

The brief arrives, the work is drawn, and the pieces are made for a particular space, occasion, or gathering.

The studio works across textile, metal, ceramic, wood, and surface applications — in small numbers and over longer timelines.

Weddings and private events

Return gifts, welcome objects, table programmes, ritual objects — designed around a single occasion in a single house.

Corporate gifting

Considered gifting for senior teams, partners, and clients. Custom motifs, quiet packaging, and objects made to stay beyond the event.

Hospitality, interiors, and private commissions

Surfaces drawn for a defined room or programme — a hotel suite, a restaurant table, a residential bedroom, a private cycle of gifting.

The brief is set with the architect, owner, or host.
